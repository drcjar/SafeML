Determination

 

I find and determine in terms of the Fatal Accidents and Sudden Deaths Inquiry (Scotland) Act 1976 as follows:-

 

In terms of Section 6(1)(a) that Danielle Welsh died at The Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh at 10.25am on the 24th June 2008.

 

In terms of Section (1)(b) that the cause of her death was

Liver failure due to

Paracetamol toxicity due to

Iatrogenic paracetamol overdose.

 

In terms of Section 6(1)(c) I find that the reasonable precautions whereby her death might have been avoided were as follows:- .

If Dr Das had checked the British National Formulary before prescribing intravenous paracetamol for Danielle on the evening of 17 June 2008.

 

If the nursing staff who administered intravenous paracetamol to Danielle between 17 and 20 June had checked that they knew the normal dosage of the intravenous paracetamol to be administered by them having regard to Danielle's weight

 

If the Pharmacist Lesley Murray had checked the British National Formulary in respect of intravenous paracetamol when reviewing Danielle's drug Kardex in Ward 67.

 

 

In terms of Section 6(1)(d) from the evidence adduced, I find that there were no defects in the system of working which contributed to the death:

 

 

In terms of Section 6(1)(e), from the evidence adduced I find that there was at the time of the death, a prevailing culture of assumed familiarity with the administration of intravenous paracetamol, a familiarity derived from the common use of oral paracetamol. That assumed familiarity was misplaced.

Purpose of a fatal accident enquiry

 

[3] Legal Framework

 

The purpose of the Inquiry in terms of the Fatal Accident and Sudden Deaths Inquiry (Scotland) Act 1976 is for the Sheriff to make a determination setting out the following circumstances of the death, so far as they have been established to his satisfaction-

 

(a) Where and when the death and any accident resulting in the death took place;

(b) The cause or causes of such death and any accident resulting in the death;

(c) The reasonable precautions, if any, whereby the death and any accident resulting in the death may have been avoided;

(d) the defects, if any, in the system of working which contributed to the death or any accident resulting in the death;

(e) any other facts which are relevant to the circumstances of the death (all in terms of Section 6(1) of the Act)

 

[4] The Court proceeds on the basis of evidence placed before it and although described as an Inquiry, the Sheriff's powers generally do not go beyond making a determination in relation to the circumstances established to his satisfaction by evidence following upon investigation by the Procurator Fiscal and any other party if so advised.

 

[5] This inquiry is not the proper forum for determination of civil liability or of negligence and any findings in any such inquiry should avoid connotations of negligence (See Black -v- Scott 1990 SLT 612 and Dekker 2000 SCLR 1087).

 

[6] Mr Stephenson's written submission included a very helpful passage on the powers and limitations on these powers which the sheriff exercises in a Fatal Accident Inquiry and which makes reference to the Report of Findings of the review of Fatal Accident Inquiries, an independent enquiry by Lord Cullen published in November 2009.

[7] Lord Cullen considered the role of the sheriff in his final Report. With particular reference to recommendations he expressed the following views:-

 

3.28 Taking these questions in turn, it is, in my view, unsatisfactory that there is uncertainty as to the power of the sheriff to make recommendation arising out of his or her findings, or as to the potential scope for such recommendations. I am in no doubt that the sheriff should be able to make recommendations directly related to the circumstances of the individual death. At the same time I consider that there is considerable force in the arguments against the sheriff making recommendations of general application, to which I have referred...

 

3.29 It would be inappropriate, in my view, for an FAI to be treated as if it were a public enquiry taking a nationwide approach and calling for far greater resources. For a sheriff to over-reach what could be supported from his evidence would detract from the respect which his or her recommendations deserve

 

[8] It was submitted for the Health Board that the practice of making recommendations, while it is an established one, may in fact be unnecessary in the light of the statutory findings that the Sheriff is required to make. It was submitted that any recommendations should be recommendations directly related to the particular circumstances of the case and any such recommendations should recognise the limitations of the procedure and the evidence heard and avoid "over-reach". I agree with that general approach.

 

[9] Accordingly in framing this determination I have in mind the restrictions on the enquiry and the powers to make recommendations.

 

[10] Similarly, having regard to the submissions of the Health Board I adopt Sheriff Kearney's observations in his determination in relation to the death of James McAlpine, issued on 17 in January 1986, and referred to at paragraph 8-99 of the 3rd edition of Sudden Deaths and Fatal Accident Inquiries by Ian Carmichael. Sheriff Kearney observed:-

"In deciding whether to make any determination (under section 6(1)(d)) as to the defects if any in any system of working which contributed to the death or any accident resulting in the death, the court must, as a precondition to making any such recommendation, be satisfied that the defect in question did in fact cause or contribute to the death. The standard of proof and rules of evidence (apart from the consideration that evidence did not require to be corroborated) is that applicable in civil business (1976 Act section 4(7)) and accordingly the standard of proof is that of the balance of probabilities."

"In relation to making a finding as to the reasonable precautions, if any, whereby the death and any accident resulting in the death might have been avoided (section 6(1)(c)) it is clearly not necessary for the court to be satisfied that the proposed precaution would in fact have avoided the accident or the death, only that it might have done, but the court must, as well as being satisfied that the precaution might have prevented the accident or death, be satisfied that the precaution was a reasonable one."

[11] Sheriff Kearney went on to say:-

"The phrase 'might have been avoided' is a wide one which has not, so far as I am aware, been made the subject of judicial interpretation. It means less than 'would, on the probabilities have been avoided' and rather directs one's mind in the direction of the lively possibilities."

Background

 

[i] Danielle was born on the 5th of June 1989. She had an undefined condition; her sister Catherine is the only other diagnosed case in Scotland. This condition gave rise to spondyloepiphyseal dysplasia, short stature, mild but longstanding learning difficulties, problems with hearing and chronic pain particularly in the limbs and joints. Danielle had for some time been under the care of inter alia Dr John Currie at the Royal Hospital for Sick children, Yorkhill. He is a consultant paediatric anaesthetist with an interest in pain relief. If the normal analgesics did not assist, then Danielle would see him. At June 2008 she weighed 35 kilogrammes.

 

[ii] Given Danielle's age, the management of her case was in the process of being transferred from Royal Hospital for Sick Children at Yorkhill to the Southern General Hospital (SGH) . She was waiting to be seen by the Pain Clinic at SGH

 

[iii] At June 2008 Danielle was on a regular regime of prescribed medicines which included Tramadol and paracetamol. These were taken orally, although on occasion Danielle did not like taking pills.

 

[iv] Paracetamol is usually supplied in the form of tablets (oral paracetamol). It can also be administered per rectum or intravenously. The standard adult therapeutic dose for oral paracetamol is 1g four times daily. This is also the standard therapeutic dose in children from the age of 12. Medical professionals including all doctors, pharmacists and nurses are generally familiar with the use of oral paracetamol, and aware of the standard dose..

 

[v] Whereas paracetamol given orally has been in widely used for a period in excess of fifty years, intravenous paracetamol has been a more recent addition to the range of analgesics available to the medical profession. It had begun to be used in post operative care but the ease of use, the lack of side effects and the apparent familiarity of the drug meant that its use became more widespread. The sole manufacturer is Bristol-Myers Squibb Pharmaceuticals Limited (Bristol-Myers) who market their product under the name Perfalgan. The intravenous paracetamol given to Danielle was Perfalgan.

 

[vi] Paracetamol in excess can be toxic to the liver and may result in acute liver failure leading to death. Voluntary drug overdoses often involve ingestion of paracetamol. The clinical manifestation of paracetamol toxicity is delayed and may only manifest up to 96 hours after ingestion. Again doctors, pharmacists and nurses are likely to know this. This has to be seen in context. In day to day hospital practice, drugs that could be toxic in overdose are used, many of which are as or more intrinsically dangerous than paracetamol.

 

[vii] The consequences of an overdose of paracetamol are as follows; in the standard dose of paracetamol, approximately 90% is conjugated with Glucuronides and other molecules suitable for renal excretion. The remaining 10% or so is metabolised by Cythchrome P450 enzyme 2E1 into a toxic metabolite called NAP QI. This is normally rapidly conjugated with Glutathine and is then neutralised and safe for excretion. Glutathine stores in the liver are relatively short-lived requiring regular supplementation by feeding. As the amount of Paracetamiol in the blood increases, the metabolism by the P450 2E1 pathway increases exponentially as the Glucoronidation pathway has a fixed ceiling; every increase above the standard dose gives rise to a disproportionately toxic effect

 

[viii] Paracetamol is dealt with in the Clinical Handbook which was in use at the time as follows:-

"Paracetamol is an effective analgesic for mild to moderate pain and improves the effect of other analgesics in the treatment of moderate and severe pain...All patients should be offered it regularly to a maximum dose (1g four times daily) by the oral or rectal route, prescribing it in the regular prescriptions section of the Prescription form." There is no mention of an intravenous prescription.

[ix] The intravenous dose of paracetamol is dealt with in the British National Formulary No 55 ("BNF") as follows, inter alia, at page 227:-

"By intravenous infusion over 15 minutes ADULT and CHILD over 50 kg, 1g every 4-6 hours, max 4g daily; ADULT and child 10-50 kg, 15mg/kg every 4-6 hours, max 60mg/kg daily".

 

[x] The perfalgan vial used by the hospital has a label which, after listing the ingredients, says the following:

"See enclosed leaflet

Do not store above 30°C

Do not refrigerate or freeze

Keep out of the reach and sight of children

Restricted to adults and children weighing more than 33kg

Please see leaflet for other information."

 

[xi] The leaflet referred to will rarely if ever be with the individual vial.

 

[xii] At the time of Danielle's admission, all staff who dealt with her considered that they were familiar with paracetamol. The general level of knowledge was to the effect that there was no distinction between the two modes of administration i.e. oral and intravenous in terms of the amount prescribed. There was no widespread awareness or knowledge of the distinction between Intravenous and oral administration of paracetamol, and the consequent difference in effect. Oral administration of medicine is preferable to intravenous administration. In the ordinary course, any medicine which can be administered orally will be preferred and the oral route resumed as soon as practicable

 

[xiii] Against that background Danielle found herself in the Southern General hospital.

 

[xiv] Timeline

 

15th June 2008

 

Danielle was increasingly confused argumentative and aggressive. This was out of character. She complained of feeling unwell. Her parents were sufficiently concerned to take her to the SGH. They presented at about 23.23. She was seen by a triage nurse at the Accident and Emergency Department and then by the locum registrar, Dr Bardham. Danielle was referred to the on call Specialist registrar. She was found to be responsive but very anxious, with confused speech. Her temperature and blood pressure were normal. A number of laboratory tests were carried out, disclosing raised white cell count which might point to infection. The initial concern was encephalitis or meningitis. A brain scan was arranged with a view to a lumbar puncture if that was negative.

 

[xv] 16th June 2008

The CT scan was normal. Danielle was seen by Dr Wallace again about 03.45. A lumbar puncture was attempted but could not be effected. An MRI brain scan was considered but Danielle's condition, where she continued to be agitated, precluded that as the patient requires to be still. The presumptive diagnosis was addressed by the administration of anti-viral and antibiotic drugs as shown by the Kardex at p 51 A B &C

 

[xvi] Danielle's height and weight were not entered on the Kardex, although there is a space for weight; (P51) However, in gremio of the hospital notes, Dr Wallace recorded Danielle's weight and prescribed the drugs "according to weight/paediatric doses." Danielle was also prescribed 1g paracetamol "as required". The route of prescription was given as "IV/PO", meaning either intravenously or orally. The prescriber of that drug cannot be identified. It must have been prescribed before 6.45am. This appears at p 56 of the Kardex. The prescriber appears to have equiparated the oral and intravenous doses of paracetamol.

 

[xvii] At 10.30 am Danielle was seen in ward 20 by Dr Semple.

Danielle was transferred to Ward 21 where she remained until the 19th June

The "as required" paracetamol was stopped during the course of the 16th June.

 

 

[xviii] 17th June 2008

 

Danielle remained on Ward 21; she was seen by a number of junior doctors. That ward was normally the responsibility of Lorraine Perry, a clinical Pharmacist; she was not on duty and her responsibilities fell upon Saima Afzal, who reviewed Danielle's Kardex. She was aware, (although she could not remember why) that Danielle's weight was 35kg and consulted both the adult and paediatric BNF to confirm that the antibiotic prescription was appropriate. Danielle was noted to be vomiting by the evening. Oral administration of drugs was accordingly more difficult. She continued to be in pain.

 

[xix] Dr Das was on duty. She was then a foundation year one doctor . This was some time around 10.00pm. She was asked, probably by way of a post-it note on the Kardex, to prescribe pain relief and anti-emetic drugs. She had had no previous dealings with Danielle; she did not know her weight. She proceeded to prescribe on the basis that Danielle was an adult.

 

[xx] She had not used intravenous paracetamol before; her decision was informed by a number of factors as follows:-

The patient had previously had oral paracetamol;

There was a previous prescription for intravenous paracetamol (albeit as an alternative to Oral;

She regarded herself as familiar with paracetamol (albeit not intravenous administration);

She recalled that it was a nursing suggestion;

She was prescribing in an adult hospital;

The practice was to revert to oral administration at the earliest opportunity; any prescription would be subject to review.

She did not see Danielle; she did not know her weight; she did not check the BNF. She did not know the dosage. She did not know that it was weight dependent.

In the event, the dosage prescribed, 1g four times daily, was in excess of the appropriate dosage for someone of Danielle's weight. She should have received 525mg per dosage.

 

[xxi] Danielle received 2 doses of the prescribed IV paracetamol in the evening. An MRI scan was attempted but abandoned due to significant agitation. It was determined that she should have an MRI scan and a lumbar puncture in the Institute of Neurological Sciences at ward 67 SGH.

 

[xxii] 18th June 2008

Danielle was admitted to ward 67 for the administration of the MRI and lumbar puncture under general anaesthetic, as well as for general assessment. This transfer was necessary as these procedures could not be undertaken in any other ward. The intravenous administration of paracetamol continued.

 

[xxiii] 19th June 2008

Danielle remained in ward 67; for the purposes of the enquiry the care was not noteworthy. A lumbar puncture was performed and was normal. The intravenous paracetamol continued. An MRI scan was normal. Her white cell count was still high, but the medical staff were able to discount any neurological disease. The diagnosis otherwise was still unclear. Further tests were undertaken.Danielle's Kardex was reviewed there by Pharmacist Lesley Murray. She may not have seen Danielle in person. She did not alter the prescription. She did not know that the intravenous and oral dosages of intravenous paracetamol were different. She assumed that they were the same. She did not check. The need for intravenous rather than oral painkillers continued with Danielle still on a drip; she was prescribed a second, additional, anti-emetic for nausea in the course of the evening, showing that she continued to be nauseous, and undermining the utility of oral administration of paracetamol.

 

[xxiv] 20th June 2008

Danielle was still agitated. Danielle was transferred back to ward 25; this was a different medical ward. By this time the combination of infection indicators and the identification of what appeared to be "crackles" on the lungs gave rise to a possible diagnosis of atypical pneumonia. As a result of this, her prescription of Acyclovir was stopped as this was an antiviral drug no longer felt to be addressing the condition.

 

[xxv] 21st June 2008

There are no medical notes or nursing notes for the 21st June The clinical notes go from the 20th June to 22nd. No tests appear to have been undertaken

 

[xxvi] 22nd June 2008

Danielle continued to be treated for atypical pneumonia; there seemed to be an improvement noted to her demeanour. Her condition initially caused concern because her SEWS score was 4. Blood tests were taken and showed grossly abnormal Liver Function Tests. She was referred to a consultant who at 23.25 referred to the liver unit at Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh (RIE). There was considerable concern about Danielle's fitness for transfer.

 

[xxvii] 23rd June 2008

At 01.30 Danielle was transferred by ambulance to Surgical ITU with acutely deranged Liver Function tests with coagulopathy and raised paracetamol levels. Danielle was in a critical condition so was intubated and ventilated to provide a degree of stability for the transfer, arriving at 03.45 am. She was stabilised .She was considered for Transplant but having regard to the appropriate criteria, she was considered to be unsuitable. She was considered to be likely to survive. Although ill, the prognosis was reasonable.

 

[xxviii] 24th June 2008

Danielle suffered a cardiac arrest. She was subject to intensive CPR for a period of 35 minutes or so; she appeared to recover but was very unwell. She then suffered another heart attack and was pronounced dead at 10.25 am.

It was recorded that Danielle died of liver failure due to paracetamol toxicity due to paracetamol overdose.

 

 

[18] Assessment of the evidence

I heard from 20 witnesses, details of whom are given as an appendix to this determination. I am satisfied that each and every witness did their best to give truthful evidence, even where, as in the cases of Dr Das, Melanie Gale, and Lesley Murray that had the potential to implicate them in the iatrogenic overdose. It was clear to me that with the tragic and momentous exception of the paracetamol overdose, Danielle received attentive, focused and appropriate care.

 

[19] Given the consensus about the cause of death, it is not necessary to comment in detail upon the evidence. I have given a narration of the evidence in the findings in fact, but I do wish to make some observations in connection with some of the witnesses.

 

[20] In the course of his evidence, Dr Overell was asked who was ultimately responsible for the prescription. His response was to the effect that "everyone was responsible"; however that has to be tempered with his evidence that he as a consultant he rarely prescribed drugs himself; it would tend to be left to junior staff. He himself was candid enough to accept that he had not known of the weight implication; he said that although he had not reviewed the Kardex he would have regarded the intravenous paracetamol prescription as "credible."

 

[21] Dr Dillon was asked, in common with a number of the witnesses, whether any particular measure might have prevented the accident. He volunteered that the Kardex might have a separate part for intravenous drugs administered, so that this was noted. Apart from observing that in this particular case, not one of the relevant professionals regarded the intravenous route as likely to have caused any different approach, it was quite properly put to him that the variation of the method of recording any information on medical records was the provenance of a committee. Three doctors had given evidence of three different regimes in three different health boards. I do not think that is for a sheriff in the context of this sort of enquiry to innovate upon or trespass upon the recording of such information. However, I make some observations at the conclusion of this determination in deference to the points made.

 

[22] I have dealt in the context of the findings in fact at paragraphs [xix] and [xx] with Dr Das's explanation for the prescription of the intravenous paracetamol. She was plainly still affected by her role in this matter. She gave her evidence carefully and honestly and did not attempt to minimise her role, but did explain clearly the thought process that lead to the prescription.

 

[23] Melanie Gale was the only nurse to give evidence whose involvement could be detected from analysis of the signatures on the Kardex. Her recollection was not perfect; the description she gave of Danielle as giggly and smiley could not be reconciled with the other evidence about her presentation. She said that she would normally, in an unfamiliar drug, check the BNF and then the monograph. She had administered intravenous paracetamol before; it was unusual but not unprecedented. She looked at the vial; She knew Danielle was over 33 Kg, but did not know how she knew it.

 

[24] She was asked about the dosage; her response was that the vial did not give any details about the dosage. She was referred to the NMC standards for medicines management; she said that she had followed these. She said that if she had had concerns, irrespective of the prescribing history, she would have raised them or acted differently. Her position was that there was nothing in the surrounding circumstances to cause her alarm. She assumed that the intravenous and oral dose were the same. This assumption was wrong.

 

[25] Lesley Murray also assumed that the doses were the same when she reviewed the Kardex on the 18th June; she was subject to the same prevailing assumption. She did not check. It did not occur to her to check.

Paracetamol intravenously stood on its own.

That seemed to be a general observation rather than a precaution that could be imposed.

[41] She then submitted that every patient when being reviewed by either a doctor or a pharmacist should be seen. Danielle was not seen by Dr Das nor Lesley Murray. Neither were told of any issue but everybody who spoke to having a physical memory spoke to Danielle being small

She submitted that if Lesley Murray had seen Danielle and realised that she was not a standard size adult it might have occurred to her to have a closer look at the doses.

 

[43] She submitted that although there were resource issues it was a reasonable precaution

 

[44] She also made a submission that there should be an identifiable clinician who retains responsibility for a patient even if that patient is transferred from ward to ward. There was a concern about the chain of responsibility. Dr Overell indicated that everyone was responsible but that there was no clear structure responsibility

[47] Miss Adair then addressed section 6(1)(d) the defects. She thought there was a defect in Greater Glasgow Health Board approving a new formulation of a drug with which most staff were highly familiar when there was a risk associated with it. [48] She thought there was a defect in the system whereby pharmacists had not reviewed her Kardex in conjunction with seeing her. She was only seen by Ms Afzal. She thought that was a dangerous practice. She thought that a patient should be seen on a daily basis by a ward pharmacist. That had been a defect. She restated her submission that every patient being reviewed should be seen. She submitted that it was not imposing a much greater burden to have the notes and to look at them in the presence of the patient, it would address human error. She considered it was reasonable.

 

[49] She said so far as the other facts were concerned there was not a robust system of review of the prescription chart either on the ward or at transfer.

 

[50] Mr Hunter agreed with the Crown submissions in relation to formal findings that considered that there was room for the court to add that paracetamol toxicity was due to iatrogenic paracetamol overdose. He described the doctors at the Southern General as being responsible for deplorable failings. Danielle need not have died. He thought it was insufficient to call it regrettable. She entered a hospital ward with an undetermined condition possibly atypical pneumonia which she appeared to be recovering. This was successfully treated by a combination of anti-viral and antibiotic medication. At post-mortem there were no signs of any other significant organ damage causing death.

 

[51] He submitted that Dr Das had simply got it wrong. She did not deny that. She expected a review of dosage. That was where things had gone wrong. She was unaware of the weight consequent as was everyone else. He could not necessarily agree that in all cases a patient required to be seen. The issue was her weight. The records about her weight were incomplete.

 

[52] He accepted that from the evidence of Julie McQueen it was clear that a number of changes were recommended. Danielle was seen by 11 nurses and 12 different doctors. She received 20 doses. The doctors were at all level and not one of them noted the overdose. He described it as a lamentable failure of all professional. All were responsible. There was a deplorable laxity.

 

[53] He appreciated that the Health Board could not over label the vial but suggested a recommendation to the MHRA about labelling; if this was made sufficiently clear by a label whoever was administering the dose would have identified the risk in relation to low weight. He accepted that a number of things had gone wrong but many had been addressed by the Critical Incident Review.

 

[54] He then made submissions that the antidote had been issued too late. His point was that earlier detection, for example, by the liver function tests would have meant earlier application of the antidote which was familiar.

 

[55] He thought that the inter-ward transfers and the inter-hospital transfers were areas of concern because of the lack of record. It seemed to have been addressed to an extent but the failure to identify signatures and names was unhelpful. Records were only useful if complete.

 

[56] He acknowledged there were obvious difficulties with information and education. Dr Dillon had asked a number of people; nobody had known about the intravenous paracetamol administration. In his submission the medical staff should have known anyway, but if they did not know, the vial should have informed them of the appropriate dose. He said complacency was at the root of this. The clinical handbook which junior doctors had did not give any warning. The BNF did do.

 

[57] He was concerned about the information contained in the Kardex. Her death should have been avoided. The family just wished that this would not happen again. Intravenous paracetamol itself was not the issue. It was the system around it which needed to be considered.

 

[58] Miss Donald's submissions were made in writing. She had nothing to add to these. Mr Stephenson's submissions were made in writing. He did take the opportunity to respond to the submission by the Crown.

 

[59] In the first place the Crown had suggested that intravenous paracetamol should be available in surgical wards only. He submitted that the proposal was too wide and too general and too unspecific. There was insufficient evidence. If drugs were not immediately accessible then there may be different risks and issues.

 

[60] So far as the dissemination of information was concerned, again, he regarded the submission as being vague. It seemed to rest the responsibility on the Health Board. There was no evidence to support it. It was speculative but the Health Board had a strategic role and would not have responsibility for disseminating information.

 

[61] The next issue he addressed was the weight on the Kardex. He admitted that it was clear from the evidence that other Health Boards did not have a space for weight. It was not clear what the nursing notes said about weight but the issue was not her weight but that nobody appreciated the significance of it.

 

[62] So far as the recommendation that someone reviewing the Kardex or the notes always sees the patient, it was not enough for the Crown to say that would just take a little bit longer as the resource was already there to carry out the review. There is no evidence whatsoever about whether it made any difference and what's more, such a recommendation could not be simply not beneficial but positively harmful by not addressing the gaps that would then appear in the care. He gave as an example Lesley Murray's evidence about having to provide a scoping report to justify her position as a full-time ward pharmacist at the INS in Ward 67. He submitted that it bordered on the irresponsible to submit that the court should impose a different system on the basis of the limited evidence heard.

It is a different balancing exercise as notes need to be accessible and intelligible and another layer in the Kardex forms to be signed will just increase the "paper haystack around the information needle".

 

[64] So far as the concerns about the transfer between wards and hospitals were concerned the absence of the nursing notes made it difficult to know what information was transferred. It was clear that some papers go with her.

 

[65] Finally, in relation to suggestion of a liver function test every day, this went too far. In any event there had been no evidence about why there had been no such test; no one was asked about it and it may have been as a result of the potential improvement. He submitted that it was an invasive procedure to take tests every day. It involved the taking of blood. If it is not for every patient, then the evidence would need to give a basis for determining the criteria for inclusion. Again, he considered that to make such a recommendation would over reach the function of the sheriff.

 

[66] Discussion

 

A Fatal accident enquiry always proceeds with the benefit of hindsight; it is much easier from that standpoint to identify what went wrong and what should have happened.

 

[67] However what was clear from this exercise was that there was a gap in the knowledge of all those who prescribed, administered, reviewed and considered the intravenous paracetamol prescription; none of these individuals knew that paracetamol was prescribed differently if administered intravenously and if the patient weighed less than 50kg. Each who was asked explained that they were familiar with paracetamol, that it was generally safe and that it did not occur to them to check the dose. Dr Dillon, as referred to by Mr Hunter, had conducted an informal poll amongst his staff and colleagues .

 

[68] Not one person had appreciated that the intravenous dosage had different parameters.

 

[69] For obvious reasons, the spotlight has shone with particular focus on Dr Das; she was clearly, from her evidence, still very troubled by her involvement in this overdose. Her account was intelligible in relation to her particular actions. She was left a note to prescribe an intravenous analgesic; she was familiar with paracetamol; there had been a previous prescription of intravenous paracetamol, albeit with an oral alternative; she was in an adult hospital; there was no information in the documentation which she had to draw attention to the fact that Danielle was anything other than a normal 19 year old girl; Danielle was already receiving drugs intravenously so had an intravenous line. Intravenous administration of an analgesic was short term, with the oral route being reinstated as soon as practicable. Dr Das understood that the prescription would be reviewed, as indeed it was, for example by Lesley Murray. She has been subject to criticism for her failure to consult the BNF; however her own clinical handbook did not alert her to any paracetamol concerns. Similarly, even if she had seen the label, it did not in terms highlight the prescription for someone of Danielle's weight. Dr Das did not strike me as irresponsible or complacent. On the contrary she appeared to be a diligent and conscientious if inexperienced professional, and whilst she was clearly busy on the evening of the initial prescription, that did not affect her thought process.

 

[70] Melanie Gale, whose signature was the only one apparently decipherable enough to identify, acted as a representate of all the nursing staff. She had not appreciated that there was anything unusual in the administration of the intravenous dosage so did not check the BNF. Although there were defects in her recollection she did not come across as complacent, or irresponsible and I accepted her evidence that, had she had concerns, she would have checked the position.

 

[71] Similarly, Lesley Murray is the one pharmacist who can be identified as reviewing the Kardex in the context of the ongoing intravenous paracetamol. She did not check the dose; it did not occur to her to check. Again she did not come across as irresponsible or complacent. I am satisfied that had she had concerns she would have addressed such concerns.

 

[72] There was no systemic failure identifiable from the evidence led. The failure was a prevailing and pervasive failure to appreciate the particular peculiarities of the intravenous dosage of paracetamol, a lack of knowledge and experience shared by 23 different professionals who dealt with Danielle and indeed by a number of the witnesses who gave evidence, as spoken to by Dr Dillon.

 

[73] The three precautions which I have identified are easy to determine with the benefit of hindsight; the accumulation of evidence demonstrated that no professional expressed any concern about the use, or dosage of the intravenous paracetamol. It is unfortunate to single out individual practitioners for attention when they acted intelligibly, if wrongly, given the state of knowledge which prevailed.

 

[74] There was a prevailing attitude that paracetamol was so familiar that no further enquiry was required., Danielle's presentation was unusual in terms of her weight in an adult hospital. These factors combined tragically so that she received an overdose on a sustained and regular basis, which led to her death.

[75] There was no identifiable shortage of information about the intravenous administration if the medical personnel had felt the need to check; the tragedy is that no-one did. The BNF and the monogram both gave specific information. The clinical handbook and the label did not; neither purport to be comprehensive.

 

[76] In relation to the steps to be taken I am satisfied that there are no additional recommendations which it is appropriate to make on the basis of the evidence led.

 

[77] This is for two reasons; firstly I have regard to the steps taken (contained in appendix 5 and spoken to in some detail, by the witness Julie McQueen) and the evidence that intravenous paracetamol had been withdrawn from general use; secondly I have regard to the need to guard against over-reaching my powers. This has not been a general enquiry about, for example, the practices and protocols of prescription in hospital, or the uniformity or otherwise of medical notes. These are matters which are already subject to intelligible and informed structures..

 

[78] In relation to the submissions not already dealt with about recommendations, or defects I have some observations. Miss Adair submitted that a reasonable precaution would be to include on the vial label a warning such as CAUTION IF PATIENT IS LESS THAN 50 KILGRAMES. However it was clear that the labelling of medicines is such that an ad hoc local solution is neither practicable nor desirable.

 

[79] The crown proposed that the prescriber of any drug always saw the person for whom the prescription was written. However the physical presentation of a patient is sometimes of no assistance at all to those prescribing. They may obtain much more information from blood tests, clinical notes, scans, or x-rays. Even those who had seen Danielle did not necessarily link her small stature to a need to scrutinise the dose of paracetamol. There was no evidence about the additional time that would be required for such an exercise to be undertaken.

 

[80] In relation to the Crown's suggestion that metabolic checks should be done every day, this seems to me to be trespassing entirely on the clinical judgement of the professionals involved. This is particularly so when there was, as was noted by Mr Stephenson, no explanation or exploration of why no such tests were carried out there may have been very good clinical reasons including an apparent improvement for such a position.

 

[81] Miss Adair had also addressed section 6(1)(d) in relation to whether there are any defects. She thought there was a defect in Greater Glasgow Health Board approving a new formulation of a drug with which most staff were highly familiar when there was a risk associated with it. I do not regard the evidence as disclosing any defect. There was information in the BNF and on the information sheet provided by the Health Board (at pages 305-306 0f the productions). There was no lack of accessible information. There was a failure by staff to appreciate the need to consult such information for the reasons given above.

 

[82] Miss Adair, Mr Hunter and Mr Stephenson all made comments about the transfer between hospitals, when Danielle was transferred from SGH to the RIE; the evidence was insufficient for any conclusions to be drawn but it remained noteworthy that there was uncertainty amongst the professionals about what records would be sent

 

[83] Finally, it is appropriate to record those matter which were left unresolved but which might merit some attention.

 

[84] No-one reported this death despite there being a clear obligation to do so and despite the clear impression even from the outset that Danielle died as a result of an iatrogenic overdose. The matter was not really explored in evidence, but suggests either an absence of protocol about reporting such a death, or an absence of familiarity with any such protocol.

 

[85] The Kardex system was a matter of some comment in both evidence and submission, but given the provenance of the blank medical record notes, which are prepared after consideration and consultation and which are not, perhaps surprisingly, uniform within the NHS in Scotland, it is inappropriate to make any recommendations. However, I make the following observations

 

[86] It is trite that Medical records should be accessible and intelligible; I share the concern put in a neat phrase by Mr Stephenson that parties should warn against surrounding the information needle with a paper haystack, and these observations must be put in that context.

 

[87] Dr Wallace had given evidence that the weight should have been included on the Kardex. There was space for it in the Kardex relating to Danielle. However, her experience in her role with a different health board was that the kardex did not have a space for weight. Whilst in this case I cannot hold on the evidence that the recorded weight would have alerted any clinician to the appropriate dosage, such information may have a material role in assessing dosage or route of administration. It seems to me that weight can be an important factor and should have space to be noted, and be noted.

 

[88] Similarly there is no means of recording on the Kardex the existence of unusual metabolism or condition; there was some evidence that Danielle's condition could give rise to difficulties for venous access, or rectal dosage. Consideration may be given to space being afforded for such information.

[89] There was no means available to anyone reading the Kardex of knowing whether or not the Kardex had been reviewed by ward pharmacist or any other person. Assumptions were made about reviews which could not be vouched by any record of such review. Consideration might be given to affording space on the Kardex for such a review to be recorded.

 

[90] Most signatures on the Kardex were indecipherable. I noted that the medical records from the ERI included a column for writing the name of the noter and their designation in block capitals. This did not seem to have been uniformly observed. Notwithstanding that failing, consideration might be given to such a space being uniform in all medical records, for ease of identification of the professional staff involved.

Glossary of selected medical and technical terms

 

BNF

The BNF is a joint publication of the British Medical Association and the Royal Pharmaceutical Society and provided for national circulation. The BNF is intended mainly for doctors, pharmacists and nurses in the National Health Service. It is designed as a rapid-reference digest and provides up-to-date guidance on the selection, prescribing, dispensing and administration of medicines. The BNF details all the medicines that are generally prescribed in the UK, with special reference to their indications, cautions, contra-indications, side-effects, dosage and relative costs.

 

Clinical Handbook

 

The Clinical handbook referred to in these proceedings is provided by the NHS Greater Glasgow and and C Acute services division and is a readily portable and accessible resource on evidence based routine clinical practice; it is available in the GGHB intranet. Every doctor will have a copy or very quick access to a copy either in book form or on the intranet.....

 

Kardex

 

This was formally the name for a filing system for nursing records and orders that was held centrally on the ward and contained all the nursing details and observations of patients that had been acquired during their stay in hospital. Although this system is no longer used for nursing records, since care plans are now held at the patient's bedside rather than centrally, the term 'Kardex' continues to be used generically, for certain patient record systems whether held centrally or otherwise; in the context of these proceedings the Kardex refers to the record of drugs administered to Danielle.

 

NMC Standards for medicines management book

 

The NMC is the Nursing and Midwifery Council, which regulates the two professions of nursing and midwifery; inter alia it maintains a register of all nurses and midwives eligible to practice in the UK . It provides advice and guidance to registrants. The book seeks to "set standards for safe practice in the management and administration of medicines" by registrants , and provide "the minimum standard by which practice should be conducted and...provide the benchmark by which practice is measured."

 

Paracetamol

 

Paracetamol is an analgesic, thereby relieving pain, and an antipyretic , whereby it lowers fever. The trade name of the intravenous paracetamol product used in the case is perfalgan. Perfalgan is indicated for the short-term treatment of moderate pain, especially following surgery and for the short-term treatment of fever, when administration by intravenous route is clinically justified by an urgent need to treat pain or hyperthermia and/or when other routes of administration are not possible.

Effect of overdose; There is a risk of liver injury (including fulminant hepatitis, hepatic failure, cholestatic hepatitis, cytolytic hepatitis), particularly in elderly subjects, in young children, in patients with liver disease, in cases of chronic alcoholism, in patients with chronic malnutrition and in patients receiving enzyme inducers. Overdosing may be fatal in these cases.

Facts

1.      Danielle Welsh (Danielle) was born on 5 June 1989. She died in the Intensive Care Unit of the Scottish Liver Transplant Unit at the Royal Infirmary of Edinburgh (RIE) at 10.25 on Tuesday 24 June 2008 aged 19. [Section 6(1)(a)] She was transferred to the RIE from the Southern General Hospital, Glasgow in the early hours of Monday 23 June 2008 suffering from fulminant liver failure. Danielle had been an inpatient at the Southern General Hospital following her presentation at the Accident & Emergency Department of the Hospital late on Sunday 15 June 2008.

2.      For the purposes of the Inquiry I accept the evidence of Professor Burt and Dr Dillon as to cause of death. Accordingly the cause of Danielle's death was paracetamol toxicity caused by a cumulative overdose of intravenous paracetamol prescribed and repeatedly administered in Wards 21, 67 and 25 of the Southern General Hospital between the evening of Tuesday 17 and Sunday 22 June 2008. This resulted in Danielle developing acute liver failure by 22 June 2008, and suffering a terminal event in the form of a series of heart attacks at the RIE on the morning of 24 June 2008. I also accept the unchallenged evidence of Dr Dillon (in chief and as modified in cross-examination on being advised of the liver function results from 20 June) that had IV Paracetamol been stopped and, if necessary, Danielle treated for paracetamol overdose, at any time up to 20 June 2008 it is likely she would have survived. In the event it was not appreciated that she had received an overdose until 22 June, by which time despite appropriate treatment in the Southern General Hospital and the RIE, events proved it was too late to avoid her death. It appears to follow from Dr Dillon's evidence that on the balance of probabilities no act or omission occurring on 21 and 22 June 2008 caused or materially contributed to Danielle's death. [Section 6(1)(a) and (b)]

3.      Danielle's parents are John Welsh and Margaret McInally or Welsh. Danielle had two siblings, an older sister Catherine and a younger brother, Martin. Mr and Mrs Welsh have attended throughout the Inquiry, showing considerable dignity and restraint. It is clear that Danielle's death has had a considerable impact on them. I would take the opportunity to convey to them my sympathy for their loss and that of Greater Glasgow Health Board. I hope that the Inquiry will ultimately provided some closure for them.

4.      Danielle had congenital condition giving rise to longstanding learning disability, hearing impairment, short-sightedness, spondylo-epiphyseal dysplasia (a disorder resulting in abnormal bone formation and short stature), and delayed puberty. Her sister Catherine suffers from the same genetic syndrome. At the time of her death Danielle was 3 feet 11 inches tall. Although at autopsy she was noted to weigh 41.9kgs this was in part due to fluid-loading caused by necessary and appropriate medical treatment during the last 36 hours or so of her life. Her normal weight was 35kgs [kilograms to pounds conversion factor 2.2046; 35kgs x 2.2046 = 77.161 lbs = 5.5115 stones]. It would have been obvious to anyone who saw Danielle that she was of short stature and (although not underweight for her height) might well weigh less than 50kgs [kilograms to pounds conversion factor 2.2046; 50kgs x 2.2046 = 110.23 lbs = 7.87 stones]. It was a feature of Danielle's condition that she suffered considerable pain in her hips, for which she was prescribed analgesic drugs. Despite her medical problems Danielle had a friendly out-going personality. She was socially engaged and enjoyed a good quality of life. She had a happy and loving home life with her parents and siblings.

5.      Until 2008 Danielle and Catherine were under periodic medical review at the Royal Hospital for Sick Children, Glasgow (Yorkhill); latterly by Dr Donaldson, Consultant Paedetrician, by Dr John Currie, a Consultant Anaesthetist with a special interest in pain relief and by Mr. Sherlock, a Consultant Orthopaedic Surgeon. 7.      As the focus of the Inquiry has come to be on paracetamol it should be noted at this point that the drug is a widely available non-opioid analgesic which can be obtained without prescription in the United Kingdom. It is also known as Acetaminophen. It is usually supplied in the form of tablets (oral paracetamol). It can also be administered per rectum or intravenously. The standard adult therapeutic dose for oral paracetamol is 1 g four times daily. This is also the standard therapeutic dose in children from the age of 12. [Crown Production No.9 page 298, British National Formulary, dated March 2008]. Doctors, pharmacists and nurses are likely to be very familiar with the use of oral paracetamol, and to be aware of the standard dose. Paracetamol in excess can be toxic to the liver and may result in acute liver failure leading to death. Voluntary drug overdoses often involve ingestion of paracetamol. The clinical manifestation of paracetamol toxicity is delayed and may only manifest up to 96 hours after ingestion. Again doctors, pharmacists and nurses are likely to know this. This has to be seen in context. In day to day hospital practice drugs that could be toxic in overdose are used; many will be as, or more, intrinsically dangerous than paracetamol.

8.      Whereas paracetamol given orally has been in widely used since the 1950's IV paracetamol has been marketed only in recent years. The sole manufacturer is Bristol-Myers Squibb Pharmaceuticals Limited (Bristol-Myers) who market their product under the name Perfalgan. The IV paracetamol given to Danielle was Perfalgan. Part V of the 1968 Act enables Regulations to be made governing packaging, labelling, leaflets, etc. For the Act (not fully updated) see http://www.statutelaw.gov.uk/legResults.aspx?LegType=All+Legislation&title=medicines+act&Year=1968&searchEnacted=0&extentMatchOnly=0&confersPower=0&blanketAmendment=0&sortAlpha=0&TYPE=QS&PageNumber=1&NavFrom=0&activeTextDocId=1662209

The Medicines and Healthcare products Regulatory Agency (MHRA) was set up in April 2003 being a merger of the Medicines Control Agency and the Medical Devices Agency. The MHRA is the government agency which is responsible for ensuring that medicines are acceptably safe. ("This body monitors approval mechanisms, controls licensing arrangements, and issues rapid warnings when evidence comes to light of a public danger concerning the use of a product or device.", Mason & McCall-Smith, Law and Medical Ethics, 7th edition 9.105). The MHRA is an executive agency of the Department of Health. No evidence was heard at the Inquiry as to the terms of the licence granted in respect of Perfalgan: there must be a licence even if primarily granted in another EC country. The extent to which any leaflet or packaging supplied with Perfalgen is prescribed under the applicable licence was not dealt with in the evidence.

10.  Use of medicines in the Scottish NHS depends upon acceptance for use by the Scottish Medicines Consortium. On 13 December 2004 the Scottish Medicines Consortium issued Summary of Advice No.137/04 accepting 1g/100ml Perfalgan for use within NHS Scotland [Health Board, Fourth Inventory Production 5]. On 6 April 2005 the Scottish Medicines Consortium issued Product Update No.172/05 accepting 500mg/50ml Perfalgan for use within NHS Scotland [Health Board, Fourth Inventory Production 6]. Neither gives any indication as to dosage although witness Pharmacist Fiona Walker spoke to a revision in the system of approval whereby dosage is now set out in the Summary of Advice.

11.  In order for Perfalgan to become available in the Southern General Hospital there required to be approval by the Health Board's Area Drug and Therapuetics Committee [?]. Perfalgan was approved in April 2005. There was nothing in the evidence to suggest that at the time of first introduction to the Southern General Hospital anyone had any reason to suspect that problems were likely to arise in the course of prescribers identifying dosage, or in connection with the subsequent checking of the dosage. It is not obvious that this would turn out to be the case, although events indicate it was. The contrary view might reasonably have prevailed in 2005: this was a new formulation of a familiar drug, and as with any other drug prescribers might reasonably have been expected to exercise proper professional care. Similarly other professionals (including pharmacists and nurses) might have been expected to exercise appropriate standards of care. There is no reason to conclude that Perfalgan ought reasonably in 2005 to have been singled out by the Health Board for special treatment. After Danielle's death the Health Board, having identified that a serious risk of prescriber error did exist in relation to Perfalgan took a series of steps which, in order to prove effective, eventually required a ban on the use of Perfalgan within their Medical Directorate, unless use could be specifically justified for an individual patient. (See further below.) This on any view was an extreme step. There is no evidence that it would have been reasonable for this, or any lesser measures to have been taken before Danielle's death.

12.  No attempt was made in the evidence to formally identify and precisely define the extent of a doctor's professional duty to (a) check the correct dosage when prescribing a drug, (b) to check the correct dosages of the drugs a patient under his/her care was receiving. For example no General Medical Council code of conduct applicable to prescribers was produced or referred to in evidence. It is inconceivable that a prescribing doctor is not under a professional duty to prescribe a drug only when they know that the dosage is safe and appropriate, checking the correct dosage if unsure. In the context of civil litigation and duties owed to a patient this appears to be accepted: e.g., Horton v Evans [2006] EWHC 2808, [2007] PNLR 17, and Jones Medical Negligence 4th ed., 4-108. A Health Board as a doctor's employer must be entitled to place some reliance upon the correct exercise of such professional duties. It is not suggested that such reliance can be absolute as it was apparent form the evidence that prescriber errors do occur. It appeared to be accepted by Dr Fraser and Dr Overell that there was an onus on successive doctors responsible for patient care to review a patient's continuing medication and that correct dosage was being received.

13.  Part of the role of the ward Pharmacist at the Southern General Hospital in June 2008 was to check patient drug Kardex to see that dosages were correct and that there were no likely adverse interactions between prescribed drugs. Pharmacists could also be contacted for advice when on duty, if required. Again no applicable formal code of conduct (e.g., issued by the Royal College of Pharmacists) was produced or referred to defining how a Pharmacist should go about their duties. However the checking of drug Kardex for prescriber errors was within their specific job remit.

14.  The only professional code of conduct referred to in evidence was the Nursing and Midwifery Council, Standards for Medicines Management, Crown Production No.6. The sole nurse who gave evidence was Melanie Gale. She accepted the applicability of the Standards to her and to the nurses who administered Perfalgan to Danielle at the Southern General Hospital. In particular she accepted the applicability of Standard 8 which includes the following: "You must know the therapeutic uses of the medicine to be administered, its normal dosage .... You must consider the dosage, weight where applicable, method of administration, route and timing." It is difficult to accept that the professional responsibilities of doctors and Pharmacists would fall below this standard.

15.  Therefore the system in place at the Southern General Hospital in June 2008 to protect patients, including Danielle, against iatrogenic drug overdose involved the exercise of professional care and responsibility by doctors when prescribing drugs and when reviewing patients, Pharmacists when they reviewed Kardex or responded to requests for advice, and nurses whenever a prescribed dose was administered.

16.  At first use of Perfalgen at the Southern General Hospital was confined to Surgical Wards, for post-surgical pain relief. There appears to have followed a gradual expansion in use beyond Surgical to Medical Wards. There does not appear to have been any particular reason driving this process. By June 2008 use on Medical Wards at the Southern General Hospital was established but at the fairly low level of about 2 -3 [?] patients a month. There was then some limited experience of Perfalgen among some but not all staff on the Medical Wards.

The cause of death was as certified by Dr Julie McAdam as being 1(a) Liver failure due to 1(b) paracetamol toxicity. The evidence of Dr McAdam, along with that of Professor Burt and Dr Dillon is accepted.

 

 

s6 (1) (c)

In considering reasonable precautions which may have prevented the death of Danielle, I have had regard only to those matters which relate to Dr Das, and to Dr Fraser leaving the wider picture to others.

 

It is my submission that no reasonable precautions may be suggested, in connection with the evidence given by Dr Das and concerning her actions in dealing with Danielle. The nature of Dr Das' involvement makes the implementation of any recommendations around her error difficult.

 

It is quite clear that the paracetamol was prescribed at a level which was too high for someone of Danielle's weight. It is equally clear, in my submission that none of the clinical staff were aware of the difference. There was complacency in dealing with an "old" drug such as paracetamol such that no one picked up on the initial prescribing error. There is no explanation for that, other than it would appear that the reviewers of the kardex were reassured at seeing paracetamol and did not question it at all. This despite those reviewers being well aware of Danielle's weight and need for adjusted antibiotics. Had any single person been aware of the difference between IV and oral paracetamol then the error, if it had been made in the first place, could have been picked up.

 

It may be suggested by others that a reasonable precaution would have been for Dr Das to have checked the BNF. That is a matter for your Lordship, but in my submission, whilst all the witnesses indicated that should be done when dealing with an unfamiliar drug, I do not recall any saying that they would have done it. None has said that it was unreasonable of Dr Das not to have done it, as I recall. I repeat here that for a precaution to be reasonable there had to be evidence that a party's action or omission was not reasonable and it is my submission that the evidence does not take us that far.

 

Another suggestion which I am quite sure will be raised is that a doctor should see a patient before prescribing. Whilst that almost seems trite, it is relevant again to comment that no one criticised that action - indeed I don't recall any witness being asked to comment on it. Dr Das gave clear evidence that she acted as any other junior doctor on call acts, in her experience. She had too many "routine" tasks to do, to allow her to visit all patients she was asked to prescribe for and no particular reason for visiting Danielle was flagged to her.

 

 

s6 (1) (d)

I do not propose to deal with systemic defects on behalf of Dr Das, or Dr Fraser, as it is my submission that both operated within the system in place as far as they were concerned. Others will comment on the systemic failures in this case.

 

 

s6 (1) (e)

There are no facts to note which are relevant to the circumstances of death, and connected with Dr Das other than those noted above.

 

As far as Dr Fraser is concerned, she did note that if Danielle had had a less fragmented path through her hospital stay then the error relating to the paracetamol may have been picked up earlier. It may be relevant therefore to note that a more appropriate path for Danielle to have followed would have been to send her back to Dr Fraser from Ward 67, once the neurology team had dealt with the investigations they required to assist with. This may be something which your Lordship wishes to comment on here.

 

 

Conclusion

In conclusion therefore, I invite the court to make formal findings under s6 (1) (a) and (b) of the Act, in relation to both Drs Fraser and Das, and thereafter if his Lordship considers it appropriate to make any further findings about their actions in that period in June 2008, to record those under s 6 (1) (e).
